Hackathon Teams

Our “hackers” are 25 current and recent JHU graduate students from the MS in Data Analytics and Policy, MS in Applied Economics, and MA in Government programs. Hackers are organized into five teams, each with a different substantive focus connected to our broader event theme.

Transparency in Government

This theme explores how governments can make decisions, budgets, and performance visible in ways that feel meaningful and trustworthy to residents. It centers on closing the promise–performance gap by pairing clear reporting with honest communication about uncertainty, trade-offs, and implementation progress. It also grapples with how privacy and security feature in transparency policy. The aim is to show how transparency, when designed for comprehension and accountability, can invite engagement rather than fuel cynicism.

Energy and Climate Policy

This theme explores building institutional credibility through tangible improvements in affordability, reliability, and resilience, especially for communities facing high energy burdens or the effects of the climate crisis. The work brings together assistance program design, reliability disclosures, and targeted infrastructure investments, linking technical outcomes to perceived fairness and competence. The story is one of energy and climate policy that delivers visible benefits while communicating results in ways people can understand and trust.

Social and Economic Justice

This theme examines how institutions can reduce disparities in access, outcomes, and treatment across health, justice, education, and employment. It looks for biased processes and bottlenecks that quietly shape inequity, and considers how transparency, accountability, and community co-design can shift both performance and perception. The narrative is about building systems that not only function better but also feel fair, respectful, and responsive to those they serve.

Democracy and Misinformation

The focus here is on safeguarding civic participation by improving information integrity and voter understanding amid polarized media and digital manipulation. It considers interventions like source labeling, content provenance, bot detection, and neutral issue summaries, aiming to increase knowledge without overwhelming or biasing users. The broader arc is a democratic ecosystem where rules are clear, data privacy is respected, and citizens can make informed choices with confidence.

Policy Evaluation and Public Trust

This theme is about closing the loop between policy promises and lived outcomes through rigorous yet practical evaluation and feedback. It emphasizes citizen-relevant metrics, lightweight data pipelines, and iterative improvements that are visible in everyday interactions with public services. The narrative highlights how clear, accessible reporting can turn evaluation into a trust-building practice that demonstrates competence and progress over time.
